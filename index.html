01First Impression

The first screen sets the entire tone.

Most apps lose people in the first three seconds. This opening screen pairs warm, real photography with three punchy tags — "Find new friends," "Connect with people," "In your city" — so the value and the local relevance land before a single tap. That's the kind of first-impression work I only get right when a project isn't rushed.

// Onboarding built to earn the second screen, not just the install.
02Discovery & Search

Search built around real plans, not just keywords.

Time tabs — Today, Tomorrow, Weekend — sit right next to category tabs for groups and social activities, and every event card already shows a rating, a "going" count, and a map shortcut. That's three layers of decision-making support before the user even opens the event. Details like this are what get skipped on rushed builds.

// Filter by time, category, and trust signal — all in one glance.
03Personalization Input

Interests as the entry point, not an afterthought.

Hand-drawn tag chips for Hiking, Cooking, Coding, Mountain Bike, and dozens more turn a single onboarding step into the seed for every recommendation the app will ever make. This is the same principle behind the client dashboard I build alongside every project: capture the real signal early, and everything downstream gets smarter automatically.

// What a user taps here is what a homepage learns from later.
04Post-Join Moment

The moment right after joining is the moment that matters most.

A confirmation card, a warm welcome message, and — critically — two related-group suggestions surfaced immediately, before the user has a chance to close the app and forget why they came. Most builds treat this screen as a formality. I treat it as the single highest-leverage moment in the whole flow.

// Confirmed, welcomed, and already offered the next step — in one screen.
05Beyond the Screen

The product doesn't stop at the screen.

Wine on a rooftop, a hike at sunset, a pottery studio, a group high-five — four completely different activities, curated so every kind of user sees themselves in it. This is what makes a lifestyle app feel authentic instead of generic: the imagery has to earn the promise the copy is making.

// The app's job is to get out of the way once people actually meet.
06Retention Layer

Retention lives in the inbox.

Unread badges, read receipts, and group threads that survive long after the event ends — this is the screen that decides whether someone opens the app again next week or forgets it exists. Getting messaging right is unglamorous, invisible work, and it's exactly where I spend the time other builds don't have.

// The event ends. The conversation, and the reason to come back, doesn't.
07Trust at Scale

Scale becomes the pitch itself.

"Join over 60 million members," backed by real, diverse faces instead of stock icons, does more to convert a hesitant install than any feature list could. Knowing when a number should be the hero of the screen — not buried in a footnote — is a design decision, not a copywriting one.

// Nothing sells an app faster than proof other people already trust it.
